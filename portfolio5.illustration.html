PHISH MAYAN POSTER 2015
The hieroglyphs of the ancient Maya is one of the worlds most beautiful forms of writing, however, stone carvings often yielded sloppy lines and hard-to-read glyps. I thought it deserved a makeover. My goal for this project was to design a Mexico themed screen-printed concert poster for the fans the Rock and Roll superstars Phish. I wanted to capture the beauty of the ancient Maya writings, while invoking a feeling of modernity, elegance, and relaxation.

DEMOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS

My first step is demographic analysis. From a poll of 2,438 Phish Fans or "Phans," I learned that 44.5% are 25-34 years old, and 27.2% are 35-44 years old. I also learned that over 80% of Phans are male, and over 50% have seen at least 31 total shows.

I gathered that my target audience would be the young professional male with an extreme emotional connection and loyalty to the band. They are likely very familiar with Phish lyrics and themes. While the typical Phan is likely inspired by nostaligic printing methods like screen-printing, they have graduated from the classic "dorm-room" Rock and Roll art to a more mature and classier "family living-room" style.

MAYA GRAPHIC RESEARCH

Next, I researched Maya glyphs, architecture, art, and culture. I also researched todays interpretations of Maya style art. I discovered that the Maya Calendar and "Hunab Ku," were drastically over-represented. My goal was to find graphics that were recognizably mayan, without cliche overused themes. It was important for me to create a unique rendering.

CLEAN IT UP

I transformed a collection of graphics with familiar Maya themes and unique content to invoke a feeling of modernity and elegance. I performed this by redrawing the images using graph and tracing-paper, utilizing clean 90 degree angles. I then scanned and patiently retraced the images using Adobe illustrator. Many of the redrawn graphics looked too modern, so I created a border radius on the 90 degree angles to find the perfect balance of traditional Maya and unique modern style.

EMOTIONAL CONNECTION

Next, I found pictures of the band members to transform into Mayan Glyphs. Including the Phish members in a unique way would inspire the feeling of emotional connection to the band that has provided Phans so much creative influence. I also created glyphs for some of Phish's most popular songs that include a Mexican theme including "Lizards, Sloths, and Vultures."

FINAL PRODUCT

I studied the demographics, researched Maya culture, and transformed their design to connect with Phish Phans. I printed the art using gold foil to represent the ancient elegance of the Maya and the feeling of a relaxing sunny vacation. The matte black and white backgrounds created contrast and class, while the glossy glyphs in the background keep the eye busy without impeding on the gold subject matter. The poster was a huge success. I sold 400 prints at $50 a piece, and many patrons spent hundreds more for appropriate framing.

PHISH MAYAN REDESIGN
For the second year in a row Phish returned to Mexico for a 3 night festival. The 2015 poster was a huge success. My goal was to improve upon the themes used in the previous years print. I would continue to use Mayan glyphs to represent Mexico, but I would also incorporate elements of the Mexican flag and the Aztec Empire. The flag recalls the legend of an eagle sitting on a cactus while devouring a serpent that signaled to the Aztecs where to found their city. I would use the imagery of the sanke to convey an even deeper cultural representation.

SAME SAME BUT DIFFERENT

The snake theme incorporated a stronger cultural representation and it also inspired a circular movement that the previous linear version lacked. The challenge would be to include new Maya and Aztec subject matter within the snake graphic. I began by drawing the outline of a very curvy snake. Within the snake were 5 border lines used to separate colors, add depth, and act as guidlines to scale complex Maya and Aztec graphics.

LOGO

The Maya and Aztec Calenders are overused and cliche. My goal was to incorporate these cultures most innovative discovery, time, using innovative graphics. The circular image combined with the 13 separate graphics clues the user to the classic Mayan calender, however, it is not immediately recognizable. Patrons might also discover that the symbols under "Mexico" are the dates of the event spelled using Mayan glyphs. It is important to me that patrons discover new inspiring elements beyond their first experience with the print triggering an "ah-ha" moment long after the print is framed on the wall.

FINAL PRODUCT

I added an original linear background at the top of the print and a familiar background of gylphs at the bottom as a "throwback" to the original design. The linear style helps the curvy foreground stand out as the primary subject matter. Next, I chose blue, orange, and sea-green to represent the relaxing colors of the vacations clear blue skies, bright sun, and crystal clear water. I used sparkle foil and lava foil papers to emphasize the sparkle of the sky and sun, and the movement of the water.

GRATEFUL DEAD 50TH ANNIVERSARY
Fare Thee Well: Celebrating 50 Years of the Grateful Dead was a series of concerts which were performed by most of the surviving members of the Grateful Dead to celebrate their 50th anniversary. One of 2 performances took place at Chicago's Soldier Field on July 3, 4 and 5, 2015. These performances marked the first time Weir, Lesh, Kreutzmann and Hart had performed together since the Dead's 2009 tour and was publicized as the final time the musicians would all perform together.

DEMOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS

The core Deadhead Demographic skews older and wealthier and whiter than average, though I was a bit surprised to learn that 35-44 year-olds–people whose musical tastes were formed in the 1980s and 1990s–are as likely to know and love the Dead as their Boomer elders.

I gathered that my target audience would have an extreme emotional connection and loyalty to the band. They are likely very familiar with lyrics and themes. While the typical Deadhead is likely inspired by nostaligic printing methods like screen-printing, they have graduated from the classic "dorm-room" Rock and Roll art to a more mature and classier "family living-room" style.

GRATEFUL GRAPHIC RESEARCH

Next, I researched Grateful Dead themes and lyrics. I learned of the arsenol of Grateful Dead symbology recognized throughout the culture including, the skull and skeletons, roses, sunflowers, dancing turtles and bears, the thirteen point bolt, any many more. Each symbol tells its own story with folkloric Grateful Dead lyrics. The danger in using Grateful Dead symbology is the risk of being cliche, so I was on a mission to put my own twist on the classics.

CHICAGO

Another important piece of any concert poster is location. I researched Chicago extensively and realized that hotels nearest the venue would be across the street from North Chicago's iconic bean. This would be a perfect centerpiece to represent the city and would be displayed perfectly using foil paper. I also researched the weather for the weekend and discovered that there would be a full moon. I wanted to connect Chicago's symbols with that of the Grateful Dead. The bean reflects a distorted landscape of the city, just as the moon represents a larger-than-life skeleton's distorted vision of a skull. The roses in the background represent one of the Grateful Deads most iconic symbols, and also double as an abstract symbol for Chicago's nickname, "The Windy City."

FINAL PRODUCT

I carefully reconstructed the city in Adobe Illustrator, before adding color. I would use red an blue to represent the colors of America on July 4th, one of the dates of the concert. I would use foil paper once again to capture the reflective properties of the bean.

JERSEYS: THE COLLECTOR CULTURE
Completing successful projects with Phish helped me dive even deeper into their demographic. One of the most important discoveries is that Phans fall into a collector culture. They love unique products, especially those that are one-of-a-kind. My first introduction to the collector culture is when patrons began asking for signed and numbered prints with their favorite number. Strangely enough, I realized that some folks have an unbelievably strong emotional attachment to "their number." I also discovered that Phans love sports. There is an interesting correlation between the consistency of a bands "tour" including the statistics of songs played, how well they played them, and where it was played vs. the same consistency in sports statistics. As a result, I created the Phish Basketball Jersey.

GOTTA CATCH 'EM ALL

All the jersey team names reference a song name, and the names on the back reference a character from that song. The colors of the jerseys correlate with New England Basketball Teams, where the majority of Phans reside, and each one is numbered 1-99 to encourage individuality and inspire the collector culture. No two jerseys are the same. Once all of the jerseys have sold, I release the “away team” version. Specifically numbered jerseys are currently being traded online for premium prices generating hype and publicity.

JERSEYS EVERYWHERE

The jerseys were such a success that bands and festivals across America adopted the idea. I continued to Make jerseys for STS9, The Motet, Wakarusa Festival, and The Mockinbird Foundation, and Grassroots California. Hundreds of limited release items often sold out within minutes. To date, I have sold over 2000 jerseys retailing at $80. Later I connected with a direct wholesaler in Asia to reduce the production cost from $35 to $8. This allowed me to continue mass sales of jerseys at a lower price with a much higher profit margin.

WRIGLEY
The goal of this concert poster was to celebrate the magic of Wrigley field. Wrigley's most iconic symbol is the ivy covered brick outfield wall. I thought the magic could be captured by covering a majestic woman in Ivy. Wrigley is also one of the oldest stadiums so I captured its vintage feel by including old ticket stubs for the title area and where subtle detail was needed.
BAKERS DOZEN
The "Baker's Dozen" may be Phish's most triumphant accomplishment. They played 13 total sold-out shows in just over two weeks at Madison Square Garden without repeating one song. In order to match their efforts, I had to go "BIG!" Sloths are beloved in the Phish community due to their hit song "The Sloth," so I included giant sloths climbing to the top of the Chrystler building to eat one of the "Baker's Dozen" donuts. Since the Chrystler Building is an icon of the Art deco movement, I choose to use that style to embelish the print outside of its landscape.
STS9
STS9, short for Sound Tribe Sector Nine, is a band with a demographic much different than Phish and the Grateful Dead. The band presents itself as "from a different planet," and many of its song titles and album names are "outer space" themed. Their lastest album titled "A Universe Inside" correlated strongly with the concerts venue, The Aragon Ballroom, which celebrates the night sky with a massive painted star scape.
GAMEHENDGE
Trey Anastasio, the front man for Phish, wrote an album for his senior thesis titled, "The Man Who Stepped into Yesterday." The subject matter of the album regards a disgruntled vet transporting through a portal to "Gamehenge" or "The Land of Lizards." He must save the Lizards by climbing a mountain to meet their God name "Icculus" who proclaims that the Lizards must "read the book." As silly as it sounds, the songs within his thesis are regarded as very special in the Phish community. Below are posters representing the majestic Lizards, and their God, Icculus.
